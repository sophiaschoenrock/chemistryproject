2.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

Chemical bonds describe how atoms are held together. AP expects you to classify bonding using electronegativity (ΔEN), distinguish intramolecular vs intermolecular interactions, and connect bond type to physical properties.

Ionic, covalent, metallic (overview)

  • Ionic: electron transfer forms cations and anions; attractions in a lattice dominate.
  • Covalent: electron sharing; nonpolar (very small or zero ΔEN) vs polar (moderate ΔEN) covalent bonds.
  • Metallic: delocalized valence electrons among metal cations (developed further in 2.4).

Intramolecular vs intermolecular

Intramolecular forces act within a molecule (or within an extended covalent network formula unit): covalent bonds, and the forces that define that species’ connectivity. Intermolecular forces (IMFs) act between separate molecules or formula units: London dispersion, dipole–dipole, hydrogen bonding, ion–ion in lattices (context-dependent language).

Electronegativity and bond polarity

Larger ΔEN between bonded atoms → more ionic character in the bond model; small ΔEN → covalent. A dipole moment vector points from partial positive (δ+) toward partial negative (δ−).

Network covalent vs discrete molecules

In quartz (SiO2), strong covalent bonds build a three-dimensional network: there are no separate small “SiO2 molecules” in the usual particulate picture of the solid. That is different from CO2(s), where dispersion forces act between distinct CO2 molecules. On exams, match the model to the structure type before you label forces.

% ionic character (qualitative)

A smooth curve relates ΔEN to percent ionic character. There is no single magic cutoff: treat “mostly ionic” vs “polar covalent” as a continuum when the problem gives electronegativities or dipole data.

Language: Say “predominantly ionic” or “polar covalent” when the question gives EN data—avoid treating bonding as only two discrete boxes when ΔEN is intermediate.

If a stem shows two identical atoms bonded, the bond is nonpolar covalent; if it shows H-bonding between molecules, that is intermolecular.

2.2 Intramolecular Force and Potential Energy

Bond formation lowers the system’s potential energy relative to separated atoms. Bond energy, bond length, and potential-energy curves are linked: stronger bonds often have higher bond dissociation energies and shorter lengths (for comparable bond orders).

Potential energy vs internuclear distance

A typical curve has a minimum at the equilibrium bond length. Pulling atoms closer than that minimum raises energy (nuclear repulsion); pulling them apart raises energy until the bond breaks.

Bond energy and length

  • Bond dissociation energy (BDE): energy to break one mole of a particular bond in the gas phase (context matters on FRQs).
  • For the same two elements, a shorter bond is usually a stronger bond (higher BDE) when comparing single bonds to single bonds.
  • Bond order (single vs double vs triple) strongly affects both length and strength.

Endothermic bond breaking, exothermic bond making

Breaking bonds always costs energy on a bond-energy diagram (moving up the curve from the minimum toward separated atoms). Forming bonds from atoms releases energy as the system slides into the potential well. Hess-style arguments often add BDEs for bonds broken (positive contribution) and subtract BDEs for bonds formed (energy released).

Interpreting distance–energy sketches

The equilibrium separation Re sits at the bottom of the well. If a diagram labels two distances, the deeper minimum belongs to the stronger bond only when you are comparing the same bond order and a fair pair of species—read the axis labels before you rank.

Conceptual minimum: separating bonded atoms from equilibrium → higher U until bond breaks.

Use “potential energy minimum at Re” language when interpreting distance–energy diagrams.

2.3 Structure of Ionic Solids

Ionic compounds form crystalline lattices of alternating cations and anions. Stoichiometry reflects charge neutrality; lattice energy captures how favorable it is to assemble the solid from gas-phase ions (Coulombic reasoning).

Lattice features

  • High melting points and brittle fracture (ions shift and repel) compared with many molecular solids.
  • Each ion is surrounded by oppositely charged ions; coordination number depends on structure type (NaCl vs CsCl patterns, etc.).

Lattice energy trends (qualitative)

For similar structures, smaller ions and higher magnitudes of charge tend to increase lattice energy magnitude (more exothermic formation from ions). Coulomb’s law reasoning (shorter distance, larger q) is the AP justification.

Formula unit vs “molecule”

NaCl(s) is summarized by the formula NaCl because the crystal has a 1:1 stoichiometry of ions, not because NaCl units float around as neutral diatomics. Use “formula unit” language for ionic solids; reserve “molecule” for species with discrete covalent connectivity in context.

Born–Haber mindset (Unit 2 level)

A Born–Haber cycle links atomization, ionization, electron affinity, and lattice formation. You may not assemble a full cycle on every MCQ, but recognizing that lattice energy fits into an energy loop helps you sign-process arguments (e.g., large exothermic lattice formation stabilizes an ionic salt).

Particulate drawings: Show regular repeating arrays; avoid drawing “molecules” of NaCl in the solid when the question is about the lattice model.

2.4 Structure of Metals and Alloys

Metals are often modeled as cations in a sea of delocalized electrons. That model explains conductivity, malleability, and luster. Alloys modify properties by introducing other atoms into the metallic structure.

Metallic bonding

  • Delocalized electrons are mobile → electrical and thermal conductivity.
  • Layers can slip past one another without losing attraction → malleability and ductility.

Alloys

  • Substitutional: solute atoms replace host atoms (similar atomic radii help).
  • Interstitial: smaller atoms fit in holes between host atoms.

Packing vocabulary (introductory)

Metals crystallize in efficient packing (e.g., face-centered cubic, body-centered cubic). You rarely derive layer sequences on a timed MCQ; instead, connect “close-packed layers can slip” to malleability and connect packing density to typical metallic properties compared with brittle ionic lattices.

Contrast metals (delocalized e⁻) with ionic solids (localized ions in fixed sites) when explaining conductivity in the solid state.

2.5 Lewis Diagrams

Lewis structures track valence electrons: bonds as shared pairs, lone pairs as dots. They are a bookkeeping tool that you refine with formal charge and resonance (2.6) and connect to geometry (2.7).

Steps (typical)

  1. Count total valence electrons (adjust for charge on ions).
  2. Choose a reasonable skeleton (often least EN central, except H).
  3. Distribute electrons: single bonds first, then complete octets on outer atoms, then place remaining electrons on central.
  4. Form multiple bonds if central atom lacks an octet (where appropriate).

Octet rule (scope)

Second-row elements often obey the octet rule; expanded octets appear for some third-row-and-below centers in AP examples. Know your reference problems and course emphasis.

Polyatomic ions in Lewis work

Treat SO42−, NO3−, NH4+, etc. as units with known charges when counting valence electrons. Choose skeletons that place the least electronegative plausible central atom (often in the oxyacid/oxyanion families, that is still a structured rule set—follow the prompts your item gives).

Radicals and odd electron counts

Some species (e.g., NO) have an odd total valence count. You represent them with one unpaired electron in the Lewis picture; AP may test that you recognize the odd count rather than force an impossible octet on every atom.

Resonance preview: If you can draw multiple valid Lewis diagrams that differ only in π-bond placement, the species is often treated with resonance (see 2.6).

2.6 Resonance and Formal Charge

Resonance means the real electronic structure is a hybrid of Lewis contributors—not rapid switching. Formal charge helps you rank contributors and pick reasonable atom placements in skeletons.

Formal charge

FC = (valence e⁻ for the free atom) − (nonbonding e⁻ on the atom) − ½(bonding e⁻ assigned to that atom)

  • Sum of formal charges = overall charge of the ion or 0 for a neutral molecule.
  • Prefer structures with FC closest to zero; negative FC on more electronegative atoms is often favored.

Resonance hybrid

Bond lengths in a hybrid (e.g., carbonate) are intermediate between single and double if π bonding is delocalized. The College Board may ask you to explain why experimental bond lengths are equal in such ions.

Ozone vs carbonate (why both “need” resonance)

O3 is a neutral molecule with two equivalent O–O linkages in the real structure; no single Lewis line structure matches that symmetry without π delocalization. CO32− has three equivalent C–O bonds experimentally; three contributors that rotate the double bond predict that equality in the hybrid. Use the same vocabulary—delocalized π density—for both cases.

Never describe resonance as the molecule “alternating” between structures in time at ordinary conditions—that is a common distractor.

2.7 VSEPR and Hybridization

VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) predicts three-dimensional shape from electron domains around a central atom. Hybridization (sp, sp², sp³, and extensions) matches the number of σ domains used in the AP hybridization narrative.

Electron domains

  • Count σ bonds and lone pairs on the central atom as domains (double/triple bonds count as one domain toward geometry in the usual AP treatment).
  • Domain count → electron-domain geometry; lone pairs modify molecular geometry names and bond angles.

Common mappings (introductory)

  • 2 domains → linear → sp (typical).
  • 3 domains → trigonal planar → sp² (typical).
  • 4 domains → tetrahedral → sp³ (typical).

Five and six electron domains

Five domains → trigonal-bipyramidal electron geometry. Lone pairs occupy equatorial sites first to minimize 90° lone-pair–bonding-pair interactions. That logic gives molecular shapes such as seesaw (AX4E) and T-shaped (AX3E2).

Six domains → octahedral electron geometry; placing two lone pairs trans explains square-planar molecular geometry (AX4E2, e.g., XeF4 in the usual AP example set).

Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs → angles often compress (e.g., H2O vs CH4).

Hybridization limits: Use the course’s expected model; for expanded octets, d-orbital participation in hybridization may or may not be emphasized—follow your teacher’s AP-aligned framework.

Practice the chain: Lewis → domain count → VSEPR name → bond angles → hybridization of the central atom.
