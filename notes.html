1.1 Moles and Molar Mass

The mole connects the macroscopic scale (grams) to the particle scale (atoms, molecules, ions). AP questions often chain mass → moles → atoms or use stoichiometric ratios from a formula.

What is a mole?

One mole is Avogadro's number of specified entities: NA ≈ 6.022 × 1023 mol−1. You can have a mole of atoms, a mole of molecules, a mole of formula units, or a mole of ions—always specify the entity.

Molar mass

Molar mass M (units g/mol) is the mass of one mole of substance. For elements, use the periodic table value (average atomic mass for the natural isotopic mixture). For a compound, add contributions from each element: multiply each element's molar mass by its subscript in the formula, then sum.

moles = mass (g) ÷ molar mass (g/mol)

number of particles = moles × NA

Using chemical formulas

Subscripts in a molecular formula give atom ratios. In C6H12O6, each mole of glucose contains 6 mol C, 12 mol H, and 6 mol O. Use these ratios to convert moles of compound → moles of a given element, or the reverse.

Hydrates

Hydrated ionic salts are written with a dot and integer water molecules, e.g. CuSO4·5H2O. The molar mass of the hydrate includes the mass of the water of hydration.

Significant figures: AP expects answers to reflect the precision of given data. In multi-step conversions, carry extra digits internally and round the final answer to the appropriate precision.

Be fluent with dimensional analysis: cancel units deliberately so you can spot inverted conversion factors.

1.2 Mass Spectra of Elements

Mass spectrometry separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio. For elements, the pattern of peaks reveals isotopes and is used to compute a weighted average atomic mass.

Isotopes

Isotopes of an element share the same atomic number (protons) but differ in mass number (protons + neutrons). Different isotopes have nearly identical chemistry but different masses.

Weighted average atomic mass

The value on the periodic table is the weighted average of natural isotopic masses, where weights are fractional abundances (fractions that sum to 1).

Average atomic mass = Σ (isotopic mass × fractional abundance)

If abundances are given as percentages, convert each to a decimal before multiplying.

Reading a mass spectrum

  • Each peak corresponds to an ion with a particular mass (often the molecular ion M+ or fragment for molecules; for elements, peaks for each isotope).
  • Relative peak height or area reflects relative abundance of that species.
  • Most abundant isotope corresponds to the largest peak (for many elements).
Charge: The x-axis is mass-to-charge (m/z). For most AP plots, z = +1, so the readout is effectively mass.

Practice computing average atomic mass from two or three isotopic masses and percent abundances—both directions (given average, think conceptually).

1.3 Elemental Composition of Pure Substances

You relate mass percent of elements to empirical and molecular formulas. Combustion analysis is a classic method for obtaining carbon, hydrogen, and sometimes oxygen mass in an organic sample.

Empirical vs molecular formula

  • Empirical formula: smallest whole-number ratios of atoms in a compound.
  • Molecular formula: actual numbers of atoms per molecule; may be a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

Given the molar mass of the compound and the empirical formula mass, molecular formula = (empirical)n where n = (molar mass of compound) ÷ (empirical formula mass).

Percent composition by mass

% element = (mass of element in 1 mol compound ÷ molar mass of compound) × 100%

You can invert the logic: from % composition and a molar mass, infer atomic ratios by assuming a 100 g sample (grams of each element → moles → simplest ratio).

Combustion analysis

Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon or oxygen-containing organic produces CO2 and H2O. The mass of CO2 fixes the amount of carbon; the mass of H2O fixes hydrogen. If the compound contains only C, H, and O, oxygen mass is found by subtracting C and H masses from the original sample mass.

Pure substances vs characterization

A pure substance has a definite composition (element or compound). Mixtures (Unit 1.4) do not; this section assumes a single compound when doing formula work.

Show clear steps: mass → moles → mole ratio → empirical formula → integer multiple for molecular formula when data allow.

1.4 Composition of Mixtures

Mixtures contain two or more substances physically combined; composition can vary. Separation exploits differences in physical properties (volatility, solubility, polarity, magnetism).

Homogeneous vs heterogeneous

  • Homogeneous (solution): uniform composition at the macroscopic scale (single phase).
  • Heterogeneous: distinct regions or phases visible or separable (e.g., oil and water, sand in water).

Separating mixtures

  • Filtration: separates a solid from a liquid or gas by particle size.
  • Distillation / fractional distillation: separates by boiling point differences.
  • Chromatography: separates by differential affinity for stationary vs mobile phase (paper, thin-layer, column).
  • Magnetism: separates magnetic iron from nonmagnetic material.

Describing composition (intro)

Mass percent of a component in a mixture is the mass of that component divided by total mixture mass, times 100%. Mole fraction is moles of one component divided by total moles of all components. AP may ask you to interpret or compare these qualitatively or with simple calculations.

Know the difference between a pure substance (fixed composition for a compound) and a mixture (variable composition).

1.5 Atomic Structure and Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy orbitals described by quantum numbers. The Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule predict ground-state configurations and orbital diagrams for many atoms and ions.

Quantum numbers

  • n (principal): shell; larger n means higher energy on average and greater distance from the nucleus.
  • l (angular momentum): subshell type; 0 = s, 1 = p, 2 = d, 3 = f for a given n (with valid ranges).
  • ml: orientation of an orbital within a subshell.
  • ms: electron spin (+½ or −½).

Count orbitals: s = 1, p = 3, d = 5, f = 7. Each orbital holds at most two electrons with opposite spins (Pauli).

Electron configuration notation

Write configurations using subshell labels and superscripts (e.g., 1s2 2s2 2p4). Noble-gas shorthand is standard: [Ne] 3s2 3p3 for phosphorus.

Aufbau, Pauli, Hund

  • Aufbau: fill lowest-energy subshells first (follow the diagonal rule on the reference table when available).
  • Pauli exclusion: no two electrons in an atom share the same four quantum numbers.
  • Hund's rule: for degenerate orbitals (e.g., three p orbitals), place electrons singly with parallel spins before pairing.

Ions and exceptions

For main-group cations, electrons are removed from the highest-n subshell first (e.g., Fe: 4s before 3d for transition metals—follow AP examples). Some transition metals (Cr, Cu are common) have exceptional configurations that increase stability (half-filled and filled subshells).

Be able to write configurations for atoms and monatomic ions; use orbital diagrams for p-block elements when asked for "unpaired electrons."

1.6 Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

PES measures electron binding energies (BE) by irradiating a sample with high-energy photons and detecting kinetic energies of ejected electrons. It connects to electron configuration: subshells appear as distinct peaks.

Binding energy and the photoelectric relation

Binding energy is the energy required to remove an electron from a subshell in a given environment. In a simple model, photon energy hν = BE + KEelectron. Higher BE means electrons are held more tightly (typically core electrons vs valence electrons).

Interpreting a PES spectrum

  • Each peak corresponds to an electron subshell in a specific energy environment.
  • Position on the binding-energy axis: identifies subshells (e.g., 1s vs 2s vs 2p for a first-row element).
  • Relative peak height or area (introductory level): proportional to the number of electrons in that subshell.

Link to periodic trends

Across a period, valence electrons experience greater effective nuclear charge; valence peak positions shift (trends tie to Unit 1.7). Comparing elements can help rationalize relative positions.

Significant figures: On the exam, read graphs carefully—axis labels and relative intensities are the evidence for your reasoning.

Be ready to match PES peak counts and relative sizes to electron configurations and to predict which element has a higher binding energy for a given subshell.

1.7 Periodic Trends

Properties of atoms and ions change systematically with position in the periodic table. Effective nuclear charge, atomic size, ionization energy, and electronegativity are central to AP explanations.

Effective nuclear charge

Valence electrons are shielded from the full nuclear charge by inner (core) electrons. Effective nuclear charge (Zeff) is the net positive charge "felt" by valence electrons. Across a period, Zeff generally increases (more protons, added core electrons do not fully shield each other).

Atomic and ionic radii

  • Across a period: atomic radius decreases (stronger attraction to valence shell).
  • Down a group: radius increases (new shells, outer electrons farther from nucleus).
  • Ions: cations are smaller than parent atoms; anions are larger. Compare isoelectronic species by nuclear charge (more protons → smaller radius).

Ionization energy

First ionization energy is energy to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms. Trends generally increase across a period and decrease down a group. Exceptions exist (e.g., Group 2 vs Group 13, Group 15 vs Group 16) due to subshell stability and electron pairing.

Electronegativity

Relative ability of an atom in a bond to attract shared electrons. Increases toward the upper right (fluorine is highest on common scales). Used to predict bond polarity and sometimes acid strength in later units.

Always justify trends with Zeff, distance, and shielding—avoid "because it is on the right" without reasoning.

Practice 1.7

1.8 Valence Electrons and Ionic Compounds

Valence electrons (main-group s and p) determine chemical reactivity. Electron transfer between metals and nonmetals produces ions with noble-gas-like configurations; formulas reflect charge balance.

Valence electrons

For main-group elements, valence electrons are the s and p electrons in the outermost shell. Transition metals involve d (and sometimes s) electrons in bonding and oxidation states (more detail in later units).

Ionic bonding and charge balance

Metals lose electrons to form cations; nonmetals gain electrons to form anions. Ionic compounds are neutral overall: total positive charge equals total negative charge. Use the criss-cross method (magnitudes of charges become subscripts) to write formulas, but reduce to simplest whole-number ratios.

Isoelectronic series

Ions or atoms with the same electron configuration are isoelectronic. Compare radii by nuclear charge: more protons pull the same electron cloud tighter (smaller ion).

Lattice energy (conceptual)

Ionic solids are stabilized by strong electrostatic attractions in a crystal lattice. Smaller ions and higher charges generally increase lattice energy (more detail in Unit 3—here, know the qualitative idea).

Be able to count valence electrons from group numbers for main-group elements and write plausible ionic formulas with correct charges.
